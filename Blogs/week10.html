Week 10: Reflection on chosen reading

The selected reading for this week is Digital inequalities and why they matter. I chose this reading because we had a discussion in class about the biases that are present in the tech industry and how many people are affected by it. The reading addresses some of the inequalities that are present in the digital world. The reading mentions some of these inequalities which include race, class and gender.

Gender

Research in the past focused on identifying the gaps between genders in digital usage but recent studies aim to understand the reasons behind these gaps and their impact on outcomes like social connections, job opportunities and education. In class our lecturer Hanli, mentioned that the workplace tends to be harder for woman and sometimes becomes toxic due to discrimination regarding women capabilities. Another point was about ‘self-perception of skills’ whereas women tend to underestimate their online skills and abilities compared to men, even when skills are objectively similar which can ultimately affect actual online behavior and opportunities. Another point is that men outnumber women in IT development and design roles.

Health Care

ICT (Information and Communication Technology) is changing healthcare, especially in America. Using technology like eHealth, telemedicine and connected health help solve problems in healthcare, whereas it helps people manage their health. With regards to digital inequalities, some people have less access to digital technology and skills, which makes it harder for them using eHealth services such as telemedicine which allows patients to consult with healthcare providers using video calls or messaging apps. Research shows that factors like age, gender, race, education affect who uses eHealth. The example used is the Healthcare.gov website which had problems when it was launched because it had problems. It was hard to use for people with small screens or who weren't good with computers. This shows not everyone can easily use eHealth tools. This example reminds me of our lecturer, Hanli who mentioned the government covid-19 vaccination website, which was not designed well to accommodate multiple devices, which automatically excludes those who do not have access to the devices needed to make the website work properly.

Data Collection

The way data has been collected has changed so much in the past 20 years. There has been a decline in the engagement of people on surveys over the phone or in person interviews. Web surveys then emerged which made it easier to reach more people, but not everyone had equal access to the internet, and this basically led to those who participated, representing the whole population. Another point was that whether someone responding to a web survey depended on their education, income, and their internet connectivity. People who lived in rural areas often had less access to high-speed internet, so the surveys that rely on the internet may have not represented them well. Social media surveys were then the next solution, but an issue was that usually older people weren’t really on social media which affected who responds to the surveys on social media platforms. An example of this is Facebook and Instagram. More older people tend to use Facebook, while Instagram is more popular among younger users.
